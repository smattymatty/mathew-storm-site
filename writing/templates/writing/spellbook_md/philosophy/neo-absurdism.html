Optimizing your own imprisonment and calling it growth.

The Recursive Absurd

Neo-Absurdism is the trap between the condition and the cure.

Techno-Absurdism diagnoses the labyrinth - systems beyond comprehension, cruelty without a center. Humanist Absurdism offers the response - choosing to remain human when the system wants you to be a component. Neo-Absurdism explains why almost no one makes it from one to the other.

The trap works like this: the system produces impossible conditions, then sells you the tools to endure them. You download the productivity app. You attend the wellness seminar. You optimize your morning routine. Each act of self-improvement feels like rebellion - feels like agency - but it is the system metabolizing your suffering into fuel. You are not escaping the labyrinth. You are renovating your cell.

The cruelest part: knowing this changes nothing. The awareness of the trap is part of the trap.

Terminology:

The "new" in Neo-Absurdism is a claim to novelty. It's the recognition that something fundamental has changed in how absurdity operates.

Camus's absurd was discovered. You confronted the universe's silence and found no meaning. The confrontation was between you and the cosmos.

The neo-absurd is manufactured and then internalized. You confront a system that was built by human hands, find it grinding you down, and conclude not that the system is absurd but that you aren't good enough for it. The confrontation has moved inside. You are arguing with yourself on behalf of the machine.

Traditional self-improvement: Learning a skill, developing a capacity, growing as a person through effort and reflection.

Neo-Absurdist self-optimization: The compulsive drive to improve yourself for the system - to become a more efficient component - while experiencing that compulsion as freedom. The difference isn't the activity. It's who benefits. When you learn to code at midnight after your shift because you love building things, that's growth. When you learn to code at midnight after your shift because you're terrified of becoming obsolete, that's the trap.

⚠️

This essay is written from inside the trap it describes.

The author sat down to optimize a philosophical framework about the dangers of self-optimization. The irony is not accidental. It is the point. Neo-Absurdism cannot be written about from the outside because there is no outside.

I. The Warden You Elected

Somewhere in the twentieth century, the walls came down and the cage moved inside.

Foucault described a world of prisons, hospitals, and factories - disciplinary society, governed by the negative command: you must not. The factory whistle told you when to work. The foreman told you when to stop. The power was visible, external, and therefore resistible. You could at least identify the warden.

Byung-Chul Han argues that disciplinary society has been replaced by "achievement society." The modal verb has shifted from "May Not" to "Can." Its inhabitants are no longer obedience-subjects but "achievement-subjects" - entrepreneurs of themselves. The landscape has changed from "madhouses, hospitals, and prisons" to "fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories."

The change looks like liberation. It is not. "The disappearance of domination does not entail freedom. Instead, it makes freedom and constraint coincide."

A woman sets her alarm for 5 AM. No one told her to. Her employer didn't mandate it. She read an article about successful people and their morning routines — or she saw a video, or a podcast clip, or a thread. She runs, journals, meditates, reviews her goals, and arrives at work already having completed a full performance of productivity before she's processed a single email.

She is tired. She has been tired for years. But the tiredness itself becomes evidence of effort, and effort is the only currency that still feels real.

She is the warden. She is the prisoner. She is both, simultaneously, and that is why she cannot escape — there is no one to overthrow, no whistle to smash, no foreman to confront. The guard and the inmate share a body.

"Achievement society is the society of self-exploitation. The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out." Because there is no external master, the subject is simultaneously "prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator." Han writes: "In this society of compulsion, everyone carries a work camp inside."

This auto-exploitation is more efficient than any external domination "because the feeling of freedom attends it." The project of self-realization "turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself."

The alarm rings again at 5 AM. She does not hit snooze. Hitting snooze would be giving up. She does not question why a full-time salary requires a pre-dawn ritual of self-optimization to feel adequate. The question would not occur to her, because the system didn't demand the alarm. She chose it. That's the genius.

When the external oppressor disappears, rebellion becomes impossible — not because resistance is forbidden, but because there is nothing to resist. You cannot organize against your own morning routine. You cannot unionize against the voice in your head that says you could be doing more. The warden you elected cannot be voted out.

II. The Air You Can't Breathe

The alarm is only part of it. The deeper trap isn't what you do at 5 AM — it's that you cannot imagine a world where the alarm isn't necessary.

Mark Fisher called this capitalist realism: not an ideology you believe in but an atmosphere you breathe. Not a set of arguments but the absence of alternatives. It does not say "capitalism is good." It says "capitalism is all there is." It operates not through persuasion but through the quiet elimination of every other possibility from the horizon of the thinkable.

Fisher wrote that capitalist realism is "more like a pervasive atmosphere, conditioning not only the production of culture but also the regulation of work and education, and acting as a kind of invisible barrier constraining thought and action." Unlike fascism or Stalinism, it doesn't need propaganda. It doesn't need believers. It only needs participants.

"It is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism."

A student takes on debt for a degree. The degree is required for a job. The job is required to pay the debt. When the loop is described to her, she nods — she already knows. She has read the articles. She has seen the memes. The awareness changes nothing. She signs the loan papers anyway, because the alternative is not a different system. The alternative is no system — and she cannot afford no system, because rent is due.

This is not ignorance. It is not even compliance. It is the condition Fisher described with the most precise term in his vocabulary: reflexive impotence. You know things are wrong. You know that you know. And knowing that you know changes nothing, which makes you feel worse, which makes you less capable of action, which confirms that nothing can change.

Fisher described reflexive impotence among his students: they "know things are bad, but more than that, they know they can't do anything about it." The awareness is self-fulfilling. "Reflexive impotence is another phrase for depression, I think."

The mechanism is recursive: people know their own attitudes reinforce their inability to act, and this knowledge makes them feel worse rather than inspiring change, which makes them more depressed still.

The student is not stupid. She is not passive. She is drowning in information about drowning while drowning. Every think piece about student debt, every podcast about the broken economy, every thread about late-stage capitalism — she has consumed them all. The consumption is itself a form of participation. She scrolls through critiques of the system on a device financed by the system, during a break from work required by the system, and each piece of content that names her trap becomes another brick in it.

Fisher had a term for this too: depressive hedonia — not the inability to feel pleasure, but the inability to do anything except pursue pleasure. The numbing scroll. The twitchy, agitated consumption that fills every gap but satisfies nothing. Time sliced into digital fragments too small to hold intention.

Fisher described students in "a state of what I would call depressive hedonia... constituted not by an inability to get pleasure so much as by an inability to do anything else except pursue pleasure." There is a sense that "something is missing" — but no understanding that this absent satisfaction "can only be accessed beyond the pleasure principle."

This was written before TikTok.

The atmosphere does not argue. It does not threaten. It simply makes every alternative feel like fantasy — naïve, impractical, childish. Of course you need the degree. Of course you need the alarm. Of course you need to optimize. What else would you do? The question is rhetorical because the atmosphere has made it rhetorical.

III. Your Fault, Chemically

Here is where the mechanism and the atmosphere converge, where Han's structure meets Fisher's weather, and the trap snaps shut.

The system produces impossible conditions. Business ontology — Fisher's term for the demand that everything, including healthcare and education, be run as a business — ensures that every institution optimizes for metrics rather than humans. The nurse faces a billing system designed for profit extraction. The teacher faces rubrics that measure compliance. The student faces a credentialing industry that prices admission like luxury goods. These are not failures of the system. They are the system functioning as designed.

Then the system offers you the tools to endure it.

Fisher wrote: "Instead of treating it as incumbent on individuals to resolve their own psychological distress, instead, that is, of accepting the vast privatization of stress that has taken place over the last thirty years, we need to ask: how has it become acceptable that so many people, and especially so many young people, are ill?"

The "chemico-biologization of mental illness is of course strictly commensurate with its depoliticization." Depression becomes a chemical problem with a chemical solution. The question of why so many people are depressed — what is producing this epidemic — never needs to be asked.

A man works sixty hours a week at a job that pays forty hours of wages once you account for the commute, the unpaid prep, the emails at dinner. He develops anxiety. Not the dramatic kind — the low hum, the chest tightness, the 3 AM certainty that he is falling behind. He sees a doctor. The doctor prescribes an SSRI. The man takes it. The anxiety softens. He continues working sixty hours.

The prescription is not wrong. The anxiety is real, the chemistry is real, the relief is real. But the cause — the impossible conditions — remains untouched. The pill does not fix the sixty-hour week. It makes the sixty-hour week bearable. The system that produced the anxiety has outsourced its treatment to the individual it damaged, and the individual pays for the prescription out of the paycheck earned from the job that made him sick.

Han writes: "Depression is the sickness of a society that suffers from excessive positivity. It reflects a humanity waging war on itself." The depressive complaint — "Nothing is possible" — can only emerge "in a society that believes 'Nothing is impossible.'"

"Under auto-exploitation, people are turning their aggression against themselves. This auto-aggressivity means that the exploited are not inclined to revolution so much as depression."

Fisher named this with surgical accuracy: magical voluntarism — the belief that it is within every individual's power to make themselves whatever they want to be. If you fail, you have only yourself to blame. This is the dominant religion of achievement society, pushed by every self-help book, every TED talk, every "rise and grind" post. Depression becomes the shadow side of entrepreneurial culture — what happens when unlimited possibility meets limited reality.

And so the man downloads a meditation app. He tracks his sleep. He optimizes his diet. He reads a book about resilience. Each response is a form of what Han calls psychopolitics — power operating through the psyche itself, ensuring that "individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." The man is not being forced to meditate. He is choosing to meditate. He is helping himself.

He is building the walls of his cell from the inside and believing he is installing windows.

"Under neoliberalism, the technology of power takes on a subtle form. It does not lay hold of individuals directly. Instead, it ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, collapse into one.

"Neoliberal psychopolitics is a technology of domination that stabilizes and perpetuates the prevailing system by means of psychological programming and steering." The smartphone functions "like a rosary" — both serve "the purpose of self-monitoring and control."

The wellness industry is the trap made visible. Corporate mindfulness programs teach employees to manage the stress produced by impossible workloads — not to question the workloads. Yoga at lunch doesn't shorten the workday. The resilience workshop doesn't reduce the number of patients the nurse must process. The meditation app doesn't abolish the billing system. Each intervention treats the symptom and protects the cause.

The system that makes you sick sells you the cure. The cure makes the sickness bearable. The bearability prevents revolt. The cycle continues.

IV. The Loneliest Rebellion

The trap has one more layer, and it is the cruelest.

When suffering is privatized, so is resistance.

Every act of self-optimization is performed alone. The alarm at 5 AM is individual. The meditation app is individual. The therapy session is individual. The productivity system, the journaling practice, the morning routine — each is a solo project of self-improvement conducted in isolation. The system has atomized not just the suffering but the response to suffering.

In The Expulsion of the Other, Han writes: "In the neoliberal regime, exploitation no longer takes place as alienation and self-derealization, but as freedom, as self-realization and self-optimization. Here there is no Other as an exploiter, forcing me to work and alienating me from myself; rather, I voluntarily exploit myself in the belief that I am realizing myself."

When there is no Other as exploiter, there is also no Other as fellow sufferer. The elimination of external domination also eliminates the basis for solidarity.

The nurse who changes a billing code does it alone. She doesn't talk about it. She can't. The act survives precisely because it is invisible — because the system doesn't know it happened. If she organized, if she spoke, if she turned the individual act into a collective one, the gap would close. The system would learn. So she stays quiet, and her rebellion remains as atomized as her suffering.

This is the neo-absurdist trap at its most complete: even resistance has been privatized. The billing code, the generous reading of the rubric, the unenforced rule — these are beautiful, and they are also exactly what the system can absorb indefinitely. Individual mercy within systemic cruelty changes the outcome for one person, one file, one patient. It does not change the system. It cannot change the system, because individual acts are the only acts the system permits.

The nurse knows this. The knowledge does not help.

Fisher argued that the remedy begins with recognizing depression as political, not personal: "Since there are so many people who are depressed — and I maintain that the cause for much of this depression is social and political — then converting that depression into a political anger is an urgent political project."

"The 'mental health plague' in capitalist societies would suggest that, instead of being the only social system that works, capitalism is inherently dysfunctional, and that the cost of it appearing to work is very high."

Here is where Neo-Absurdism meets its own edge. The trap is real. The atomization is real. The privatization of both suffering and resistance is real. And the framework itself — sitting in your browser right now, being indexed, being consumed — is also an individual act performed in isolation. You are reading this alone. I wrote it alone. We are both, at this moment, engaged in the most Neo-Absurdist activity possible: self-optimizing our understanding of self-optimization.

The escape, if there is one, is not better understanding. It is not a sharper critique or a more precise vocabulary. Fisher was right that the first step is converting privatized suffering into political anger — but the trap ensures that even that conversion gets individualized. You post about it. You share the article. You add to the discourse. The discourse becomes content. The content feeds the algorithm. The loop tightens.

The only thing the system cannot metabolize is people who refuse to suffer alone. Not individual rebellion. Not optimized resistance. Not mindful revolution. Just the brute, inefficient, un-optimizable fact of people turning to each other and saying: this is not a personal failure. This was done to us. And there are enough of us to stop cooperating.

That is not this essay's argument to make. That is the work of Humanist Absurdism. This essay's work is simpler and more uncomfortable: to sit in the trap, describe the walls, and admit that describing them is not the same as escaping.

V. The Essay That Wrote Itself

I should tell you what happened while I was writing this.

I outlined the essay on a Saturday. By Sunday, I had restructured it twice, optimized the section order for "narrative flow," and spent forty-five minutes choosing between two synonyms. I caught myself evaluating whether the essay was good enough — whether it would perform well, whether it would resonate, whether it would make people think I was smart. I was optimizing.

The essay about the trap of self-optimization was being self-optimized. The framework that warns against internalizing systemic pressure as personal inadequacy was being refined by a person who felt inadequate about how well he'd articulated the concept of inadequacy.

"The first stage of a burnout is euphoria: I plunge into work euphorically until I finally collapse. I realize myself to death. I optimize myself to death."

The euphoria is the signature of the trap. It feels like purpose. It feels like meaning. It feels like freedom. It is the cage closing.

This is not a confession of hypocrisy. It is the thesis.

Neo-Absurdism cannot be written about from the outside because there is no outside. Every critique is also content. Every act of resistance is also an act of participation. Every essay about the trap is also an essay written inside the trap, by a person who wanted to write it well — which is to say, who wanted to optimize it — which is to say, who fell for it.

Camus could stand outside the absurd and describe it. He could imagine Sisyphus happy because the observation and the condition were separate. But we are the boulder and Sisyphus and the mountain and the philosopher describing the scene. There is no vantage point. There is no meta-position. There is only the recursive loop: write about the loop, notice you're in the loop, write about noticing, optimize the writing about noticing, notice the optimization.

The honest thing — the only honest thing — is to say: I know this. I wrote it anyway. Not because writing about the trap escapes the trap. It doesn't. But because the alternative — silence, withdrawal, the refusal to name the thing — is also a form of optimization. It's the optimization of purity. And purity is just another metric.

So: the trap exists. You are in it. I am in it. This essay is in it. The awareness does not help, the critique does not liberate, and the vocabulary does not save. The boulder still rolls downhill. The alarm still rings at 5 AM. The app still tracks your sleep.

What remains — and this is the narrow, desperate, unglamorous thing that remains — is the choice to stop blaming yourself.

Not to stop pushing. You can't stop pushing. The rent is due. But to push without the additional cruelty of believing the pushing is your fault. To suffer within the system without the bonus suffering of believing you deserve it. To be exhausted without concluding that your exhaustion proves your inadequacy.

That's not freedom. But it's the first crack in the wall — and through the crack, if you look, you can see other people pushing their own boulders, alone, believing what you believed: that the weight is personal.

It isn't.
